What This Is:

This project explores the design and implementation of a wearable near-eye computing system that transforms a single-board computer into a fully functional desktop environment displayed through dual near-eye optics.

Rather than relying on a phone or laptop, the system presents a hands-free desktop experience that supports common tasks such as browsing, media consumption, and navigation through voice and gesture-based interaction.

Early prototype hardware layout illustrating core components and system architecture.

About Us

We are Electrical Engineering students at The City College of New York developing a wearable near-eye computing system as part of our senior capstone design project.

Our work focuses on exploring how compact displays, optical components, and embedded computing can be integrated into a lightweight smart glasses prototype that presents a desktop-style digital interface directly within the user's field of view. Rather than relying on traditional monitors or bulky head-mounted devices, the project aims to investigate a more practical glasses-form wearable for everyday computing tasks.

The system is designed to support hands-free interaction through voice and gesture input while maintaining awareness of the surrounding environment. Through this capstone project, we are examining the challenges of near-eye display integration, power management, and user interaction in wearable systems, while demonstrating the potential of near-eye computing as an accessible and scalable approach to immersive technology.

Hardware Components Used

Compute & Storage:

Raspberry Pi 5 (4‚Äì8 GB RAM)

The Raspberry Pi 5 functions as the central compute unit for the smart glasses system. It runs a Linux-based operating system, generates the video output sent to the near-eye displays, and processes input from connected peripherals such as the camera, microphone, and sensors. The Raspberry Pi also manages system logic, application execution, and communication between all hardware components.

microSD Card (32‚Äì64 GB)

The microSD card serves as the primary storage medium for the Raspberry Pi. It contains the Linux operating system, system configuration files, and application software required to boot and operate the smart glasses system. The storage capacity supports both system software and experimental applications during development.

Display & Optics:

2√ó Near-Eye Micro-OLED Displays

The near-eye micro-OLED displays serve as the primary visual output devices for the smart glasses system. Each display generates a high-resolution image for one eye, which is then optically redirected into the user's field of view. Their compact size and flexible ribbon connections allow them to be integrated within the glasses frame while supporting close-range viewing.

HDMI 1√ó2 Splitter

The HDMI splitter duplicates a single video output from the Raspberry Pi and delivers the same signal to both display driver boards. This enables synchronized visual output across the two near-eye displays without requiring separate video sources.

2√ó Eyepiece / Magnifying Lenses

The eyepiece lenses are used to magnify and refocus the images produced by the micro-OLED displays. By adjusting the apparent focal distance of the display, these lenses allow the virtual image to appear clear and comfortably viewable within a near-eye, glasses-mounted system.

Display Controller / Driver Boards

The display controller boards act as the interface between the Raspberry Pi and the micro-OLED display panels. They convert incoming HDMI video signals into the appropriate format required by the displays and supply the necessary control and power signals to ensure stable image rendering.

Input & Sensing:

Camera Module (Pi Camera v3 or USB)

The camera module provides visual input for the smart glasses system and is used to capture hand movement and environmental data. Mounted near the bridge of the glasses frame, the camera enables gesture-based interaction and computer vision experiments, with image data processed directly by the Raspberry Pi.

Microphone (USB or MEMS-based)

The microphone is used to capture near-field speech for voice-based interaction with the system. In the current prototype, a compact USB lavalier microphone connects directly to the Raspberry Pi to support voice commands and basic navigation. Future iterations may transition to a MEMS microphone integrated into the frame to improve form factor and power efficiency.

Optional IMU (Head Motion / Orientation)

The inertial measurement unit (IMU) provides head motion and orientation data by measuring acceleration and rotational movement. This sensor supports experimental features such as head-based cursor control, interface stabilization, and context-aware interaction. While optional, the IMU adds realism and expands the range of interaction methods available in the system.

Power & Connectivity:

USB-C Power Bank (5V output)

The USB-C power bank serves as the portable power source for the smart glasses system. It provides regulated 5V power to the Raspberry Pi, enabling untethered operation during testing and demonstrations. Using an external power bank allows the system to be powered reliably without integrating custom battery management hardware during early prototyping.

USB-C Power Cable

The USB-C power cable delivers power from the power bank to the Raspberry Pi. This connection supplies the primary operating power for the system, allowing the Raspberry Pi to distribute power to connected peripherals such as displays, sensors, and input devices.

HDMI Cables (Short / Flexible)

Short, flexible HDMI cables are used to connect the Raspberry Pi to the HDMI splitter and from the splitter to the display driver boards. Their reduced length and flexibility help minimize bulk, improve cable routing within the prototype, and support clean integration with the glasses frame.

Camera Ribbon Cable

The camera ribbon cable connects the camera module to the Raspberry Pi‚Äôs camera interface. Its thin, flexible design allows the cable to be routed through the glasses frame while maintaining a reliable high-speed data connection for image capture and processing.

Mechanical & Mounting:

The mechanical structure of the system focuses on supporting early-stage prototyping and iterative design rather than a finalized consumer form factor. The goal is to create a wearable platform that allows displays, optics, and sensors to be securely positioned while enabling rapid adjustments during testing.

Glasses Frame / Head-Mounted Support

A modified glasses frame is used as the primary structural base for the system. The frame serves as a lightweight head-mounted platform for attaching near-eye displays, optical elements, and sensing hardware. This approach allows components to be positioned relative to the user's field of view while maintaining comfort and stability during use.

Custom Mounts / 3D-Printed Brackets

Custom-designed mounts and 3D-printed brackets are used to secure and align individual components within the frame. These mounts enable precise positioning of lenses and displays, support sensor placement, and provide mechanical stability while allowing for rapid redesign and iteration as the system evolves.

Mechanical components described represent early prototyping solutions and are expected to evolve as the design is refined.

Design Direction: System Assembly

Step 1: Raspberry Pi Operating System Setup

The system will be built around a Raspberry Pi, which will serve as the central compute unit. A Linux-based operating system will be installed on a microSD card using an imaging tool prior to hardware integration. This operating system will provide the environment for display output, peripheral communication, and application execution. Once installed, the microSD card will be inserted into the Raspberry Pi, and the system will be powered on to verify successful boot and basic functionality.

Step 2: HDMI Video Output Configuration

After the Raspberry Pi is operational, its HDMI output will be used as the primary video interface for the near-eye displays. A short HDMI cable will connect the Raspberry Pi to an HDMI 1√ó2 splitter. This splitter will duplicate the video signal, allowing identical video output to be delivered to two independent display subsystems.

Step 3: Display Driver Board Integration

Each output of the HDMI splitter will be connected to a dedicated display driver board. These driver boards function as the interface between the HDMI video signal and the micro-OLED display panels. The driver boards will handle video signal conversion, display timing, and power regulation required for stable operation of the displays.

Step 4: Micro-OLED Display Connection

Each micro-OLED display panel will be connected to its corresponding driver board using a flexible ribbon cable. These ribbon connections enable compact routing within the glasses frame while maintaining reliable electrical contact. The displays will be mounted inside the frame near the temples, where they can be paired with optical components while minimizing obstruction of the user's direct line of sight.

Step 5: Optical Subsystem Assembly

Optical elements, such as magnifying lenses or prisms, will be positioned in front of each micro-OLED display. These optics will redirect and focus the display image into the user's field of view, creating the perception of a virtual image located at a comfortable viewing distance. The alignment between the display and optics will be adjusted to optimize clarity, focus, and viewing comfort.

Step 6: Camera and Vision Sensor Integration

A Raspberry Pi camera module or compact USB camera will be mounted near the bridge of the glasses frame to capture a forward-facing view. This camera will be connected to the Raspberry Pi through either a dedicated camera ribbon interface or a USB port. The camera will provide visual input for gesture recognition and environment sensing, with image data processed directly on the Raspberry Pi.

Step 7: Additional Sensor Integration (IMU)

An optional inertial measurement unit (IMU) will be incorporated to capture head motion and orientation data. The IMU will be mounted within the glasses frame and connected to the Raspberry Pi using a low-speed digital communication interface such as I¬≤C. This sensor data will support experimentation with head-based interaction and display stabilization techniques.

Step 8: Audio Input Integration

A compact USB microphone will be connected to one of the Raspberry Pi's USB ports to enable voice input. During early prototyping, the microphone may be positioned externally to ensure clear audio capture, with the option to integrate it more tightly into the frame in later iterations.

Step 9: Power Supply and Distribution

Power will be supplied using a portable USB-C power bank capable of delivering regulated 5V output. The power bank will connect directly to the Raspberry Pi via a USB power cable. Once powered, the Raspberry Pi will supply power to all connected peripherals, including the HDMI splitter, display driver boards, camera, microphone, and sensors, ensuring a centralized and simplified power distribution architecture.

Step 10: Mechanical Integration and Cable Routing

All electronic components will be mechanically secured using a glasses frame and custom mounts or 3D-printed brackets. Internal cables, including HDMI, ribbon, and power connections, will be routed through the temples of the frame to minimize clutter and maintain balance. Care will be taken to reduce strain on connectors and preserve user comfort.

Step 11: System Validation and Iteration

Once the system is fully assembled, functional testing will be performed to confirm successful video output on both displays, proper camera operation, sensor communication, and stable power delivery. Observations from testing will guide iterative adjustments to both the mechanical layout and electrical integration to improve reliability and usability.
